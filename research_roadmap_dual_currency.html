1 Project Overview

1.1 Title (Working)

“Credit Risk and Dollarization in Cambodia: A Dual-Currency Analysis Using Interest Rate Spreads (2014–2024)”

1.2 Research Questions

  1. How do credit risk dynamics differ between USD and KHR lending in Cambodia’s banking sector?
  2. Which currency segment is more sensitive to financial shocks, particularly the COVID-19 pandemic?
  3. Can a composite dual-currency Credit Risk Index serve as an effective early warning tool for monitoring financial stability?

1.3 Why This Topic Is Strong

  • Original: No existing study applies a dual-currency CRI framework to Cambodia
  • Policy-relevant: Cambodia is actively pursuing de-dollarization — your results speak directly to whether that changes the risk profile
  • Data-feasible: Uses only publicly available NBC interest rate data (no loan-level data needed)
  • Technically impressive: Stochastic modeling, comparative analysis, stress testing
  • Interview-ready: Shows you understand Cambodia’s unique banking landscape deeply

1.4 The Big Idea in One Paragraph

Cambodia’s banking sector is unique because it operates in two currencies simultaneously — roughly 80% of lending is in USD and 20% in KHR. This means credit risk may behave very differently depending on which currency a loan is denominated in. By modeling the interest rate spread (loan rate minus deposit rate) separately for each currency using a mean-reverting stochastic process, you can construct currency-specific Credit Risk Indices and a system-wide composite index. Comparing how these indices behave — especially during the COVID-19 shock — reveals whether dollarization amplifies or dampens credit risk, and which segment serves as a better early warning signal.

  • Banking assets have grown to over 180% of GDP
  • The sector is a critical engine of economic growth but also a potential source of systemic risk

The dollarization challenge:

  • Cambodia is one of the most dollarized economies in the world
  • Approximately 80% of loans and deposits are in USD
  • The NBC has been gradually pushing de-dollarization (higher reserve requirements for USD, promoting KHR use)

The data gap:

  • Granular loan-level default data is not widely available in Cambodia
  • Traditional credit risk models (PD, LGD, EAD) require this data
  • Interest rate spreads offer an indirect but powerful alternative — banks embed their risk assessment into the rates they charge

Your contribution:

  • First study to construct a dual-currency Credit Risk Index for Cambodia
  • Uses only publicly available data from NBC
  • Provides a practical early warning tool
  • COVID-19 serves as a natural stress event to validate the framework

2.3 Section 3: Methodology (5–6 pages)

2.3.1 3.1 Interest Rate Spread Definition

For each currency \(c \in \{USD, KHR\}\):

\[ S_t^c = r_{\text{loan},t}^c - r_{\text{deposit},t}^c \]

This gives you two time series: \(S_t^{USD}\) and \(S_t^{KHR}\).

Why model them separately: Risk factors differ across segments, so models should be built separately. USD lending is influenced by global dollar conditions and Fed policy, while KHR lending is more sensitive to domestic monetary policy and exchange rate movements.

2.3.2 3.2 The Ornstein-Uhlenbeck Model

For each currency \(c\), the spread follows:

\[ dS_t^c = \kappa^c(\theta^c - S_t^c)\,dt + \sigma^c\,dW_t^c \]

Parameter Meaning What to Compare Across Currencies
\(\theta^c\) Long-run average spread Is the “normal” spread higher for KHR or USD?
\(\kappa^c\) Speed of mean reversion Does one currency recover faster from shocks?
\(\sigma^c\) Volatility Which currency has more volatile spreads?

The spread at time \(t\) follows a normal distribution:

\[ S_t^c \sim \mathcal{N}\left(m^c(t),\; v^c(t)\right) \]

where:

\[ m^c(t) = \theta^c + (S_0^c - \theta^c)e^{-\kappa^c t} \]

\[ v^c(t) = \frac{(\sigma^c)^2}{2\kappa^c}\left(1 - e^{-2\kappa^c t}\right) \]

2.3.3 3.3 Parameter Estimation

For discrete monthly observations, the transition density between consecutive observations:

\[ S_{t_{i+1}}^c \mid S_{t_i}^c \sim \mathcal{N}\left(\theta^c + (S_{t_i}^c - \theta^c)e^{-\kappa^c \Delta t},\; \frac{(\sigma^c)^2}{2\kappa^c}(1 - e^{-2\kappa^c \Delta t})\right) \]

The log-likelihood function:

\[ \ell(\kappa^c, \theta^c, \sigma^c) = -\frac{n}{2}\ln(2\pi) - \frac{n}{2}\ln\left(\frac{(\sigma^c)^2}{2\kappa^c}(1 - e^{-2\kappa^c \Delta t})\right) - \sum_{i=1}^{n}\frac{\left(S_{t_i}^c - \theta^c - (S_{t_{i-1}}^c - \theta^c)e^{-\kappa^c \Delta t}\right)^2}{2 \cdot \frac{(\sigma^c)^2}{2\kappa^c}(1 - e^{-2\kappa^c \Delta t})} \]

Maximize using scipy.optimize.minimize (with negative log-likelihood).

NoteCross-Check with AR(1)

The OU process in discrete time is equivalent to an AR(1) model:

\[S_{t+1}^c = a^c + b^c S_t^c + \varepsilon_t^c\]

where \(b^c = e^{-\kappa^c \Delta t}\), \(a^c = \theta^c(1 - b^c)\), and \(\text{Var}(\varepsilon^c) = \frac{(\sigma^c)^2}{2\kappa^c}(1 - (b^c)^2)\).

Run a simple OLS regression of \(S_{t+1}\) on \(S_t\) and back out the OU parameters as verification.

2.3.4 3.4 Crisis Threshold

For each currency, use the 95th percentile of historical spreads:

\[ S_c^{USD} = \text{Percentile}_{95}(S_t^{USD}), \qquad S_c^{KHR} = \text{Percentile}_{95}(S_t^{KHR}) \]

Sensitivity analysis with the 90th and 99th percentiles.

ImportantImportant

Each currency gets its own threshold because their “normal” spread levels are different. KHR spreads are typically wider than USD spreads.

2.3.5 3.5 Crisis Probability

\[ P(S_t^c > S_c^c) = 1 - \Phi\left(\frac{S_c^c - m^c(t)}{\sqrt{v^c(t)}}\right) \]

2.3.6 3.6 Currency-Specific Credit Risk Indices

\[ \text{CRI}^c = \alpha \cdot \sigma^c + \beta \cdot P(S_t^c > S_c^c) \]

Use \(\alpha = \beta = 0.5\) as baseline.

2.3.7 3.7 System-Wide Composite CRI

\[ \text{CRI}_{\text{System}} = w_{USD} \cdot \text{CRI}^{USD} + w_{KHR} \cdot \text{CRI}^{KHR} \]

Weighting Approach \(w_{USD}\) \(w_{KHR}\) Rationale
Loan-share weighted ~0.80 ~0.20 Reflects actual banking system composition
Equal weighted 0.50 0.50 Gives equal attention to both segments
Time-varying Changes yearly Changes yearly Captures de-dollarization trend

6 Interview Preparation

6.1 The 60-Second Pitch

“I built a dual-currency Credit Risk Index for Cambodia’s banking sector. Cambodia is heavily dollarized — about 80% of lending is in US dollars — so I wanted to understand whether credit risk behaves differently in the dollar segment versus the riel segment. I used 10 years of interest rate data from the National Bank of Cambodia and modeled the lending-deposit spread for each currency using an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck stochastic process. From that, I estimated crisis probabilities and constructed a composite Credit Risk Index. I also ran stress tests and used COVID-19 as a case study. The framework gives regulators and banks a practical early warning tool that works without needing loan-level default data.”

6.2 Likely Follow-Up Questions

“Why did you use interest rate spreads instead of default data?” > Cambodia doesn’t have widely available loan-level default data. The spread approach captures how banks price risk in real time using only publicly available data.

“What’s the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck model?” > A mean-reverting stochastic process — it captures the idea that spreads return to a normal level over time. Three intuitive parameters: long-run average, speed of reversion, and volatility.

“What were your main findings?” > [Depends on results — frame as a story about USD vs. KHR risk dynamics]

“What are the limitations?” > Spreads capture more than credit risk (also operating costs and margins). Working with aggregate market rates, not individual bank data. Model assumes normally distributed shocks.
