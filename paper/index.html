1 Project Overview

1.1 Title (Working)

“Credit Risk and Dollarization in Cambodia: A Dual-Currency Analysis Using Interest Rate Spreads (2021–2025)”

1.2 Research Questions

  1. How do credit risk dynamics differ between USD and KHR lending in Cambodia’s banking sector?
  2. Which currency segment is more sensitive to financial shocks and macroeconomic changes?
  3. Can a composite dual-currency Credit Risk Index serve as an effective early warning tool for monitoring financial stability?

1.3 Why This Topic Is Strong

  • Original: No existing study applies a dual-currency CRI framework to Cambodia
  • Policy-relevant: Cambodia is actively pursuing de-dollarization — your results speak directly to whether that changes the risk profile
  • Data-feasible: Uses only publicly available NBC interest rate data (no loan-level data needed)
  • Technically impressive: Stochastic modeling, comparative analysis, stress testing
  • Interview-ready: Shows you understand Cambodia’s unique banking landscape deeply

1.4 The Big Idea in One Paragraph

Cambodia’s banking sector is unique because it operates in two currencies simultaneously — roughly 80% of lending is in USD and 20% in KHR. This means credit risk may behave very differently depending on which currency a loan is denominated in. By modeling the interest rate spread (loan rate minus deposit rate) separately for each currency using a mean-reverting stochastic process, you can construct currency-specific Credit Risk Indices and a system-wide composite index. Comparing how these indices behave over the post-COVID recovery period (2021–2025) reveals whether dollarization amplifies or dampens credit risk, and which segment serves as a better early warning signal.

2 Your Data

2.1 What You Have

Monthly interest rate data from the National Bank of Cambodia (NBC) covering December 2021 to December 2025 (approximately 49 monthly observations). The data includes deposit money banks’ interest rates on deposits and loans in both KHR and USD.

The NBC reports two versions of each rate:

Version What It Measures Speed of Response
Weighted Average Rate on New Amount Rates on newly issued loans/deposits that month Fast — reflects banks’ current risk pricing
Weighted Average Rate on Outstanding Amount Average rate across all existing loans/deposits Slow — diluted by legacy rates from prior years

2.2 Which Data to Use

Primary analysis: Use “Weighted Average Rate on New Amount”

For an early warning indicator, you want the series that reacts fastest to changing risk conditions. New amount rates reflect banks’ current risk assessment in real time. Outstanding amount rates smooth out and delay the signal — you’d detect a shock months later than it actually hit.

Robustness check: Also run with “Outstanding Amount”

In your robustness section, repeat the analysis using outstanding amount rates. If results are qualitatively similar, it validates your approach. If the new amount CRI gives earlier/sharper signals (which it should), that’s a finding worth reporting.

2.7 Important Note on Sample Size

Your data covers ~49 monthly observations (Dec 2021 – Dec 2025). This is a shorter sample than ideal for stochastic modeling, but it is workable for several reasons:

  • The OU model has only 3 parameters (\(\kappa\), \(\theta\), \(\sigma\)), so 49 observations provide reasonable estimation precision
  • Monthly frequency gives enough data points (compared to quarterly which would give only ~16)
  • You should acknowledge this as a limitation and discuss how results would be strengthened with longer data
  • Try to find older NBC data: If the NBC publishes historical statistical bulletins (even going back to 2015 or 2018), adding those observations would significantly strengthen your analysis. Check the NBC website statistical section or their annual reports.

3.3.2 3.2 Interest Rate Spread Definition

For each currency \(c \in \{USD, KHR\}\):

\[ S_t^c = r_{\text{Term Loans},t}^c - r_{\text{Term Deposits},t}^c \]

This gives you two time series: \(S_t^{USD}\) and \(S_t^{KHR}\).

Why model them separately: USD lending is influenced by global dollar conditions and Fed policy, while KHR lending is more sensitive to domestic monetary policy and exchange rate movements. Just as banks build separate models for different portfolio segments, you build separate CRIs.

3.3.3 3.3 The Ornstein-Uhlenbeck Model

For each currency \(c\), the spread follows a mean-reverting stochastic differential equation:

\[ dS_t^c = \kappa^c(\theta^c - S_t^c)\,dt + \sigma^c\,dW_t^c \]

Parameter Meaning What to Compare Across Currencies
\(\theta^c\) Long-run average spread Is the “normal” spread higher for KHR or USD?
\(\kappa^c\) Speed of mean reversion Does one currency recover faster from shocks?
\(\sigma^c\) Volatility Which currency has more volatile spreads?

The explicit solution:

\[ S_t^c = \theta^c + (S_0^c - \theta^c)e^{-\kappa^c t} + \sigma^c \int_0^t e^{-\kappa^c(t-s)}\,dW_s^c \]

The spread at time \(t\) follows a normal distribution:

\[ S_t^c \sim \mathcal{N}\left(m^c(t),\; v^c(t)\right) \]

where:

\[ m^c(t) = \theta^c + (S_0^c - \theta^c)e^{-\kappa^c t} \]

\[ v^c(t) = \frac{(\sigma^c)^2}{2\kappa^c}\left(1 - e^{-2\kappa^c t}\right) \]

3.3.4 3.4 Parameter Estimation

For discrete monthly observations with interval \(\Delta t = 1/12\) (one month), the transition density:

\[ S_{t_{i+1}}^c \mid S_{t_i}^c \sim \mathcal{N}\left(\theta^c + (S_{t_i}^c - \theta^c)e^{-\kappa^c \Delta t},\; \frac{(\sigma^c)^2}{2\kappa^c}(1 - e^{-2\kappa^c \Delta t})\right) \]

The log-likelihood function:

\[ \ell(\kappa^c, \theta^c, \sigma^c) = -\frac{n}{2}\ln(2\pi) - \frac{n}{2}\ln\left(\frac{(\sigma^c)^2}{2\kappa^c}(1 - e^{-2\kappa^c \Delta t})\right) - \sum_{i=1}^{n}\frac{\left(S_{t_i}^c - \theta^c - (S_{t_{i-1}}^c - \theta^c)e^{-\kappa^c \Delta t}\right)^2}{2 \cdot \frac{(\sigma^c)^2}{2\kappa^c}(1 - e^{-2\kappa^c \Delta t})} \]

Maximize using scipy.optimize.minimize (minimize the negative log-likelihood) to get \(\hat{\kappa}^c\), \(\hat{\theta}^c\), \(\hat{\sigma}^c\) for each currency.

NoteCross-Check: AR(1) Regression

The OU process in discrete time is equivalent to an AR(1) model:

\[S_{t+1}^c = a^c + b^c S_t^c + \varepsilon_t^c\]

where \(b^c = e^{-\kappa^c \Delta t}\), \(a^c = \theta^c(1 - b^c)\), and \(\text{Var}(\varepsilon^c) = \frac{(\sigma^c)^2}{2\kappa^c}(1 - (b^c)^2)\).

Run a simple OLS regression of \(S_{t+1}\) on \(S_t\) and back out the OU parameters. This is a quick way to verify your MLE results and adds credibility.

3.3.5 3.5 Crisis Threshold

For each currency, define a critical threshold \(S_c^c\) above which the spread signals crisis conditions.

Primary approach: Use the 95th percentile of historical spreads:

\[ S_c^{USD} = \text{Percentile}_{95}(S_t^{USD}), \qquad S_c^{KHR} = \text{Percentile}_{95}(S_t^{KHR}) \]

Sensitivity analysis: Also test with the 90th and 99th percentiles.

ImportantImportant

Each currency gets its own threshold because their “normal” spread levels are different. KHR spreads are typically wider than USD spreads (reflecting exchange rate risk premium), so a single absolute threshold would be meaningless.

3.3.6 3.6 Crisis Probability

For each currency:

\[ P(S_t^c > S_c^c) = 1 - \Phi\left(\frac{S_c^c - m^c(t)}{\sqrt{v^c(t)}}\right) \]

where \(\Phi(\cdot)\) is the standard normal CDF.

3.3.7 3.7 Currency-Specific Credit Risk Indices

For each currency:

\[ \text{CRI}^c = \alpha \cdot \hat{\sigma}^c + \beta \cdot P(S_t^c > S_c^c) \]

Use \(\alpha = \beta = 0.5\) as the baseline (equal weight to volatility and crisis probability). Test sensitivity with alternative weights (e.g., \(\alpha = 0.3, \beta = 0.7\) emphasizing crisis probability, or vice versa).

3.3.8 3.8 System-Wide Composite CRI

Combine the two currency-specific indices:

\[ \text{CRI}_{\text{System}} = w_{USD} \cdot \text{CRI}^{USD} + w_{KHR} \cdot \text{CRI}^{KHR} \]

Weighting Approach \(w_{USD}\) \(w_{KHR}\) Rationale
Loan-share weighted (primary) ~0.80 ~0.20 Reflects actual banking system composition
Equal weighted 0.50 0.50 Gives equal attention to both segments
Time-varying Changes yearly Changes yearly Captures de-dollarization trend

Run all three as a sensitivity analysis. Loan-share weighted is your primary result.

3.3.9 3.9 Stress Testing

Apply stress shocks to both currencies:

\[ S_t^{c,\text{stress}} = S_t^c(1 + \delta) \]

Scenario \(\delta\) Interpretation
Mild 0.10 Minor economic slowdown
Moderate 0.30 Significant financial stress
Severe 0.50 Major crisis scenario

Recompute \(\text{CRI}^{USD}\), \(\text{CRI}^{KHR}\), and \(\text{CRI}_{\text{System}}\) under each stress scenario. Compare to baseline.

Key question: Is the USD segment or KHR segment more fragile under stress? This has direct implications for de-dollarization policy.

3.3.10 3.10 Rolling Window Analysis

Since your sample period is ~4 years, instead of a simple sub-period split, use a rolling window approach to track how risk evolves over time:

  • Use a 12-month rolling window to re-estimate OU parameters monthly
  • This produces a time series of \(\hat{\kappa}_t\), \(\hat{\theta}_t\), \(\hat{\sigma}_t\) for each currency
  • Plot these to show how the risk dynamics change over your sample period

This approach is better than splitting a 4-year sample into sub-periods (which would give too few observations per period).

NoteWhy Rolling Window Instead of Sub-Period Split

The previous roadmap suggested splitting data into pre-COVID / COVID / post-COVID periods. With data starting in Dec 2021, you’re already in the post-COVID recovery period. A rolling window captures the gradual evolution of risk without requiring arbitrary cut points, and it works better with shorter samples.

However, you can still annotate key events on your charts:

  • 2022: Global monetary tightening (Fed rate hikes affecting USD lending)
  • 2023: Cambodia’s post-COVID recovery acceleration
  • 2024–2025: Recent developments in NBC policy and banking sector

3.4.2 4.2 Data Selection Justification

Explain clearly (this shows methodological rigor):

Why New Amount rates: New amount rates capture banks’ current risk pricing. When a bank perceives rising credit risk, it immediately raises rates on new loans. Outstanding amount rates are diluted by legacy loans issued months or years earlier, making them slow to respond. For an early warning indicator, responsiveness is essential.

Why Term Loans and Term Deposits: Term loans are the most standard lending product — they have a fixed maturity and their pricing most directly reflects credit risk assessment. Similarly, term deposits are the best maturity match for term loans, avoiding the liquidity-driven pricing of demand and saving deposits.

3.6.3 6.3 Limitations

Be honest — this shows academic maturity:

  • Sample size: ~49 monthly observations is workable but not abundant. Results should be validated as more data becomes available.
  • Post-COVID only: The data starts in late 2021, so you don’t observe the initial COVID shock. Results reflect the recovery period rather than a full crisis cycle.
  • Spreads capture more than credit risk: Operating costs and profit margins are embedded. Changes in spreads don’t purely reflect credit risk.
  • Aggregate data: NBC rates are market averages across all banks. Individual banks may behave differently.
  • Model assumptions: The OU model assumes normally distributed shocks and constant parameters (within each estimation window). Reality may include jumps and regime changes.
  • No direct validation: Without loan-level default data, you can’t directly validate CRI against actual defaults.

4 Complete Model Summary

Step Formula Run for Each Currency?
1. Compute spread \(S_t^c = r_{\text{Term Loans},t}^c - r_{\text{Term Deposits},t}^c\) Yes (USD + KHR)
2. Fit OU model \(dS_t^c = \kappa^c(\theta^c - S_t^c)dt + \sigma^c dW_t^c\) Yes (USD + KHR)
3. Estimate parameters (MLE) Maximize log-likelihood for \((\kappa^c, \theta^c, \sigma^c)\) Yes (USD + KHR)
4. Cross-check (AR(1)) Regress \(S_{t+1}\) on \(S_t\), back out OU parameters Yes (USD + KHR)
5. Set crisis threshold \(S_c^c = P_{95}(S_t^c)\) Yes (USD + KHR)
6. Crisis probability \(P(S_t^c > S_c^c) = 1 - \Phi\left(\frac{S_c^c - m^c(t)}{\sqrt{v^c(t)}}\right)\) Yes (USD + KHR)
7. Currency CRI \(\text{CRI}^c = 0.5\hat{\sigma}^c + 0.5 P(S_t^c > S_c^c)\) Yes (USD + KHR)
8. System CRI \(\text{CRI}_{\text{Sys}} = w_{USD}\text{CRI}^{USD} + w_{KHR}\text{CRI}^{KHR}\) Once (combined)
9. Stress test \(S_t^{c,\text{stress}} = S_t^c(1+\delta)\), recompute CRIs Yes (USD + KHR)
10. Rolling window Re-estimate steps 2–7 on 12-month rolling window Yes (USD + KHR)

7.1 The 60-Second Pitch

“I built a dual-currency Credit Risk Index for Cambodia’s banking sector. Cambodia is heavily dollarized — about 80% of lending is in US dollars — so I wanted to understand whether credit risk behaves differently in the dollar segment versus the riel segment. I used four years of monthly interest rate data from the National Bank of Cambodia, specifically weighted average rates on new term loans and term deposits. I chose new amount rates over outstanding amount rates because they respond faster to changes in risk perception, which is critical for an early warning tool. I modeled the lending-deposit spread for each currency using an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck stochastic process, estimated crisis probabilities, and constructed three indices: a USD-specific CRI, a KHR-specific CRI, and a loan-share-weighted system composite. I also ran stress tests under mild, moderate, and severe scenarios, and used a rolling window to track how risk parameters evolve over time. The framework gives regulators and banks a practical monitoring tool that works without needing loan-level default data.”

7.2 Likely Follow-Up Questions

“Why did you use interest rate spreads instead of default data?” > Cambodia doesn’t have widely available loan-level default data. The spread approach captures how banks price risk in real time using only publicly available data. It’s an indirect measure — when banks perceive higher credit risk, they widen spreads on new loans.

“Why new amount rates specifically?” > Outstanding amount rates average across all existing loans, including ones issued years ago. They’re slow to react. New amount rates reflect what banks are charging right now — if risk perception changes today, it shows up in today’s new loan rates, not diluted by legacy positions.

“Why term loans and term deposits?” > Term loans are the most standard lending product — their pricing most directly reflects credit risk. Overdrafts are priced for flexibility, credit cards bundle convenience premiums. Similarly, term deposits are the best maturity match, avoiding the liquidity-driven pricing of demand deposits.

“What’s the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck model?” > A mean-reverting stochastic process. It captures the idea that spreads don’t wander randomly — they tend to return to a normal level driven by competition and monetary policy. Three intuitive parameters: long-run average, speed of reversion, and volatility.

“Is 4 years of data enough?” > It’s a limitation I acknowledge. The OU model has only three parameters, so 49 observations provide reasonable estimates. I validated with AR(1) regression as a cross-check and used rolling windows rather than sub-period splits to make the most of the available data. Extending with older data would strengthen the analysis.

“What were your main findings?” > [Depends on results — frame as a comparison story between USD and KHR segments]

“What are the limitations?” > Three main ones: the spread captures operating costs and margins, not just credit risk; I’m working with aggregate market rates, not individual bank data; and the sample covers the post-COVID period, so I don’t observe the initial shock itself.
