1 First: Is Your Methodology Legitimate?

Yes. Here’s the proof.

You might feel unsure because this is your first research project, but your methodology is built on extremely well-established foundations. Let me show you exactly where each piece comes from:

What You’re Doing Established By Year Used By
Mean-reverting model for interest rates Vasicek, O. “An Equilibrium Characterization of the Term Structure” — Journal of Financial Economics 1977 Every major bank and central bank in the world
The Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process Uhlenbeck & Ornstein (physics), adapted to finance by Vasicek 1930 / 1977 Standard in quantitative finance textbooks
Interest rate spreads as risk indicators TED spread, corporate bond spreads — used by the Fed, ECB, IMF Decades Central banks globally use spreads to monitor financial stability
MLE for OU parameter estimation Standard statistical method — Every quantitative finance program teaches this
Composite risk indices IMF Financial Soundness Indicators, various central bank indices — IMF, World Bank, BIS regularly publish composite risk measures
Stress testing Basel Committee on Banking Supervision 2004+ Required by regulation for every major bank

You are not inventing a new method. You are applying well-proven tools to a context (Cambodia’s dual-currency system) where they haven’t been applied before. That’s exactly what good applied research looks like.

The specific combination — using spreads + OU model + CRI + dual-currency comparison — is original in its application to Cambodia, but every individual component is mainstream finance.

2 Your Reference List (Organized by Section)

Here are the actual papers and documents you should cite. I’ve organized them by which section of your literature review they support.

2.1 Section 2.1: Credit Risk Measurement (PD, LGD, EAD)

These establish that the standard approach requires loan-level data, which motivates your alternative approach.

2.1.1 Key References

[1] Basel Committee on Banking Supervision (2006) “International Convergence of Capital Measurement and Capital Standards: A Comprehensive Framework (Basel II)” Bank for International Settlements. → This is THE foundational document defining PD, LGD, EAD. Available free at bis.org.

  • Search: Go to bis.org → search “Basel II comprehensive framework”
  • Direct link: https://www.bis.org/publ/bcbs128.htm
  • What to cite: The three-component framework (PD, LGD, EAD) for computing expected credit loss, and the requirement for loan-level historical default data.

[2] Altman, E.I. & Saunders, A. (1998) “Credit risk measurement: Developments over the last 20 years” Journal of Banking & Finance, 21(11-12), 1721-1742. → Classic overview of credit risk measurement methods. Good for establishing that traditional methods need historical default data.

  • Search Google Scholar: Altman Saunders 1998 credit risk measurement developments

[3] Crouhy, M., Galai, D., & Mark, R. (2000) “A comparative analysis of current credit risk models” Journal of Banking & Finance, 24(1-2), 59-117. → Compares different credit risk modeling approaches. Good for showing the data requirements of each.

  • Search Google Scholar: Crouhy Galai Mark 2000 credit risk models comparative

[4] IFRS 9 — International Accounting Standards Board (2014) “IFRS 9 Financial Instruments” → The accounting standard requiring forward-looking expected credit loss estimation. Relevant because Cambodian banks must comply but have limited data to do so.

  • Search: IFRS 9 expected credit loss standard

2.1.2 What to Write (2.1)

In 3–4 paragraphs, explain:

  1. Banks measure credit risk using PD, LGD, and EAD, which together determine expected credit loss (cite [1])
  2. These models require historical loan-level data with observed default events (cite [2], [3])
  3. Regulatory frameworks like Basel II and IFRS 9 mandate this type of modeling (cite [1], [4])
  4. In emerging markets like Cambodia, granular default data is often unavailable, creating a need for alternative approaches → this motivates your paper

2.2 Section 2.2: Interest Rate Spreads as Risk Indicators

This is your theoretical foundation — the idea that the gap between loan and deposit rates captures credit risk.

2.2.1 Key References

[5] Ho, T.S.Y. & Saunders, A. (1981) “The Determinants of Bank Interest Margins: Theory and Empirical Evidence” Journal of Financial and Quantitative Analysis, 16(4), 581-600. → THE foundational paper on bank interest margins. Shows that the spread between loan and deposit rates compensates for credit risk, among other factors.

  • Search Google Scholar: Ho Saunders 1981 determinants bank interest margins

[6] Angbazo, L. (1997) “Commercial bank net interest margins, default risk, interest-rate risk, and off-balance sheet banking” Journal of Banking & Finance, 21(1), 55-87. → Empirically shows that bank spreads reflect credit risk (default risk) and interest rate risk.

  • Search Google Scholar: Angbazo 1997 net interest margins default risk

[7] Maudos, J. & Fernández de Guevara, J. (2004) “Factors explaining the interest margin in the banking sectors of the European Union” Journal of Banking & Finance, 28(9), 2259-2281. → Shows credit risk as a key determinant of bank interest margins across European banks.

  • Search Google Scholar: Maudos Fernandez Guevara 2004 interest margin European banking

[8] Drehmann, M. & Juselius, M. (2014) “Evaluating early warning indicators of banking crises: Satisfying policy requirements” International Journal of Forecasting, 30(3), 759-780. → IMF/BIS study on early warning indicators for banking crises. Supports your use of financial variables as early warning tools.

  • Search Google Scholar: Drehmann Juselius 2014 early warning indicators banking crises

2.2.2 What to Write (2.2)

In 3–4 paragraphs, explain:

  1. The interest rate spread (loan rate minus deposit rate) is a fundamental concept in banking — it compensates banks for credit risk, operating costs, and profit margin (cite [5])
  2. Research has shown empirically that spreads widen when default risk increases (cite [6], [7])
  3. Credit spreads are widely used as early warning indicators for financial stress — central banks and the IMF monitor them regularly (cite [8])
  4. Therefore, tracking how spreads evolve over time captures banks’ real-time risk assessment, even without default data → this is the core insight of your paper

2.3 Section 2.3: Stochastic Models in Finance (Ornstein-Uhlenbeck / Vasicek)

This legitimizes your modeling choice.

2.3.1 Key References

[9] Vasicek, O. (1977) “An Equilibrium Characterization of the Term Structure” Journal of Financial Economics, 5(2), 177-188. → THE original paper. Introduced the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process to finance for modeling interest rates. Your model is a direct application of this.

  • Search Google Scholar: Vasicek 1977 equilibrium characterization term structure
  • This is one of the most cited papers in all of finance

[10] Cox, J.C., Ingersoll, J.E., & Ross, S.A. (1985) “A Theory of the Term Structure of Interest Rates” Econometrica, 53(2), 385-407. → The CIR model, which extends Vasicek. Cite to show you’re aware of alternatives and why Vasicek is sufficient for your purpose.

  • Search Google Scholar: Cox Ingersoll Ross 1985 theory term structure interest rates

[11] Hull, J.C. (2018) Options, Futures, and Other Derivatives (10th edition). Pearson. → THE standard textbook. Chapters on interest rate models cover the Vasicek/OU framework in detail. Good as a general reference for the methodology.

  • This is a textbook — cite it for methodological details

[12] Uhlenbeck, G.E. & Ornstein, L.S. (1930) “On the Theory of the Brownian Motion” Physical Review, 36(5), 823-841. → The original OU process paper from physics. Optional to cite but shows scholarly depth.

2.3.2 What to Write (2.3)

In 2–3 paragraphs, explain:

  1. Interest rate spreads don’t wander randomly — they tend to revert to a long-run “normal” level. This is called mean reversion. (Intuition: competitive forces and monetary policy pull spreads back to equilibrium)
  2. The Ornstein-Uhlenbeck (OU) process, introduced to finance by Vasicek (1977), is the standard model for mean-reverting financial variables (cite [9], [12])
  3. The OU model has three intuitive parameters: long-run mean (\(\theta\)), speed of reversion (\(\kappa\)), and volatility (\(\sigma\)). It can be estimated from data using Maximum Likelihood Estimation and has a known closed-form distribution, which makes computing crisis probabilities straightforward (cite [9], [11])
  4. While extensions exist (e.g., the CIR model [10] which prevents negative values), the Vasicek/OU framework is sufficient for your application because interest rate spreads are positive by nature in your data

  • Search Google Scholar: Kang 2005 dollarization good Cambodia

[16] Okuda, H. & Chea, S. (eds.) (2023) Cambodian Dollarization: Its Policy Implications for LDCs’ Financial Development Routledge. → A recent book specifically on Cambodian dollarization. Very relevant as a recent source.

  • Search Google Scholar: Okuda Chea 2023 Cambodian dollarization Routledge

[17] Samreth, S., Sánchez-Martín, M.E., & Ly, S. (2019) “Dollarization Dilemma: Price Stability at the Cost of External Competitiveness in Cambodia” World Bank Policy Research Working Paper No. 8893. → Discusses the trade-offs of Cambodia’s dollarization.

  • Search Google Scholar: Samreth Sanchez-Martin 2019 dollarization dilemma Cambodia

[18] IMF (2025) “Cambodia: 2025 Article IV Consultation — Staff Report” IMF Country Report. → The most recent IMF assessment of Cambodia’s economy and financial sector. Discusses financial stability risks and de-dollarization strategy.

  • Available at: https://www.imf.org/en/publications/cr/ → search Cambodia 2025
  • Cite for the most current data on Cambodia’s banking sector

[19] National Bank of Cambodia Annual Supervision Reports (various years). → Primary source for banking sector data, NPL ratios, and lending composition.

  • Available at NBC website

2.4.2 What to Write (2.4)

In 3–4 paragraphs, explain:

  1. Cambodia became heavily dollarized in the early 1990s following post-conflict inflows (cite [13], [14])
  2. Today, approximately 80% of bank lending is in USD, making Cambodia one of the most dollarized economies globally (cite [13], [18])
  3. The NBC has been pursuing de-dollarization through higher reserve requirements for USD and promotion of KHR use (cite [17], [18], [19])
  4. High dollarization creates specific financial stability risks: limited monetary policy independence, exposure to US Fed policy changes, and potential currency mismatches (cite [13], [14], [15])
  5. USD and KHR lending operate in somewhat separate segments — urban/formal economy in USD, rural/agricultural in KHR — with different risk profiles (cite [14], [16])

2.5 Section 2.5: Gap in Literature

2.5.1 What to Write

In 1 short paragraph:

Despite extensive research on dollarization in Cambodia and the well-established use of interest rate spreads as risk indicators, no study has:

  1. Applied the mean-reverting stochastic framework to Cambodia’s interest rate spreads to construct a Credit Risk Index;

  2. Compared credit risk dynamics across the USD and KHR lending segments;

  3. Used Cambodia’s dual-currency spread data to build an early warning indicator for financial stability.

This paper addresses all three gaps.

4 How to Search Google Scholar Efficiently

4.1 The Strategy

You don’t need to find all papers from scratch. Use the snowball method:

  1. Start with one key paper (e.g., Duma 2011 on Cambodia, or Ho & Saunders 1981 on spreads)
  2. Click “Cited by” underneath it → this shows every paper that referenced it
  3. Sort by relevance or date → find more recent papers on the same topic
  4. Check the reference list of any good paper → the papers IT cites are likely foundational ones you should also read

4.3 Quick Tips

  • Use quotes around exact phrases: "interest rate spread" not just interest rate spread
  • Use OR to broaden: "credit risk" OR "default risk"
  • Add year range in Google Scholar settings to find recent papers
  • Don’t read entire papers — read the abstract, introduction (last paragraph usually states the contribution), and conclusion. Only read methodology sections of papers that use similar methods to yours.
  • 15–20 references is enough for a personal research project

5 How Long Should the Literature Review Be?

For a personal project (not a thesis), aim for 3–4 pages. That’s roughly:

  • Section 2.1 (Credit Risk Measurement): 1/2 to 3/4 page
  • Section 2.2 (Spreads as Risk Indicators): 3/4 to 1 page
  • Section 2.3 (Stochastic Models): 1/2 to 3/4 page
  • Section 2.4 (Cambodia & Dollarization): 3/4 to 1 page
  • Section 2.5 (Gap): 1 short paragraph

The literature review is NOT the star of your paper — the results are. Write enough to show you understand the foundations and justify your approach, then move on.
