    "## 3.2 Interest Rate Spread Definition\n",
    "\n",
    "> **Framework** | **What** | **Why** | **How**\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### 🔷 Framework\n",
    "\n",
    "The interest rate spread — the gap between lending rates and deposit rates — is a classical proxy for credit risk compensation in banking. A widening spread signals that banks are demanding a higher premium for bearing credit risk, while a narrowing spread indicates easing risk conditions. This spread forms the core input series for all subsequent modeling.\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### 📌 What\n",
    "\n",
    "For each currency $c \\in \\{\\text{USD}, \\text{KHR}\\}$, we compute the credit spread as:\n",
    "\n",
    "$$\n",
    "S_t^c = r_{\\text{Term Loans},t}^{c} - r_{\\text{Term Deposits},t}^{c}\n",
    "$$\n",
    "\n",
    "where:\n",
    "- $r_{\\text{Term Loans},t}^{c}$ = weighted average rate on new term loans in currency $c$ at month $t$\n",
    "- $r_{\\text{Term Deposits},t}^{c}$ = weighted average rate on new term deposits in currency $c$ at month $t$\n",
    "\n",
    "This yields two time series — $S_t^{\\text{USD}}$ and $S_t^{\\text{KHR}}$ — each with **156 monthly observations**.\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### ❓ Why\n",
    "\n",
    "USD and KHR lending operate under fundamentally different risk environments:\n",
    "\n",
    "- **USD lending** is influenced by global dollar conditions, U.S. Federal Reserve monetary policy, and international capital flows.\n",
    "- **KHR lending** is more sensitive to domestic monetary policy, NBC reserve requirement changes, and exchange rate expectations.\n",
    "\n",
    "Just as credit risk practitioners build separate models for different portfolio segments — a principle enshrined in the Basel framework — constructing separate spreads for each currency allows us to capture these distinct dynamics rather than masking them through aggregation. Pooling the two currencies into a single spread would conflate structurally different risk premia.\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### ⚙️ How\n",
    "\n",
    "1. For each month $t$ and each currency $c$, subtract the term deposit rate from the term loan rate: $S_t^c = r_{\\text{loan},t}^c - r_{\\text{deposit},t}^c$.\n",
    "2. Inspect for missing values; interpolate linearly if gaps are isolated (≤ 2 consecutive months).\n",
    "3. Plot both spread series to verify plausibility: KHR spreads should be structurally wider than USD spreads due to the exchange rate risk premium embedded in riel lending.\n",
    "4. Store as two pandas Series indexed by date: `spread_usd` and `spread_khr`."

    "## 3.3 The Ornstein–Uhlenbeck (OU) Stochastic Model\n",
    "\n",
    "> **Framework** | **What** | **Why** | **How**\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### 🔷 Framework\n",
    "\n",
    "Interest rate spreads are not random walks — they are pulled back toward a long-run equilibrium by market forces (competition, arbitrage, monetary policy). The Ornstein–Uhlenbeck (OU) process, first introduced into finance by Vasicek (1977) for short-rate modeling, is the canonical continuous-time model for mean-reverting processes. It provides a mathematically tractable yet economically realistic description of spread dynamics.\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### 📌 What\n",
    "\n",
    "For each currency $c$, we model the spread as a **mean-reverting diffusion process** governed by the stochastic differential equation (SDE):\n",
    "\n",
    "$$\n",
    "dS_t^c = \\kappa^c\\left(\\theta^c - S_t^c\\right)dt + \\sigma^c\\,dW_t^c\n",
    "$$\n",
    "\n",
    "where $W_t^c$ is a standard Wiener process (Brownian motion), and the three parameters are:\n",
    "\n",
    "| Parameter | Symbol | Economic Meaning | Cross-Currency Comparison |\n",
    "|-----------|:------:|-----------------:|:-------------------------:|\n",
    "| Long-run equilibrium | $\\theta^c$ | The \"normal\" spread level toward which the process gravitates | Is the structural spread higher for KHR or USD? |\n",
    "| Mean-reversion speed | $\\kappa^c$ | How quickly the spread returns to equilibrium after a shock | Which currency recovers faster from credit stress? |\n",
    "| Volatility | $\\sigma^c$ | The magnitude of random fluctuations around the mean | Which currency exhibits more spread instability? |\n",
    "\n",
    "A useful derived quantity is the **half-life** — the time for a shock to decay to half its initial magnitude:\n",
    "\n",
    "$$\n",
    "t_{1/2}^c = \\frac{\\ln 2}{\\kappa^c}\n",
    "$$\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### ❓ Why\n",
    "\n",
    "1. **Mean reversion is economically natural**: Competitive forces, monetary policy, and arbitrage pull spreads back toward a long-run level. A pure random walk model (e.g., Geometric Brownian Motion) would imply that spreads drift to zero or infinity — economically implausible.\n",
    "\n",
    "2. **Analytical tractability**: The OU process has a **closed-form Gaussian transition density**, enabling exact Maximum Likelihood Estimation and analytical computation of crisis probabilities without Monte Carlo simulation.\n",
    "\n",
    "3. **Parsimony**: Only three parameters per currency are estimated from 156 observations — a well-identified, statistically stable estimation problem.\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### ⚙️ How\n",
    "\n",
    "The SDE has the closed-form solution:\n",
    "\n",
    "$$\n",
    "S_t^c = \\theta^c + \\left(S_0^c - \\theta^c\\right)e^{-\\kappa^c t} + \\sigma^c\\int_0^t e^{-\\kappa^c(t-s)}\\,dW_s^c\n",
    "$$\n",
    "\n",
    "Since the stochastic integral of a deterministic function with respect to Brownian motion is Gaussian, the spread follows a **normal distribution** $S_t^c \\sim \\mathcal{N}\\!\\left(m^c(t),\\; v^c(t)\\right)$ with:\n",
    "\n",
    "$$\n",
    "m^c(t) = \\theta^c + \\left(S_0^c - \\theta^c\\right)e^{-\\kappa^c t}, \\qquad\n",
    "v^c(t) = \\frac{(\\sigma^c)^2}{2\\kappa^c}\\left(1 - e^{-2\\kappa^c t}\\right)\n",
    "$$\n",
    "\n",
    "Implementation steps:\n",
    "1. Define the three-parameter OU model class for each currency.\n",
    "2. Derive the conditional mean $m^c(t)$ and variance $v^c(t)$ expressions.\n",
    "3. Compute the half-life $t_{1/2}^c = \\ln 2 / \\kappa^c$ as a reported diagnostic.\n",
    "4. A half-life of 6 months, for instance, means a spread shocked 2 pp above equilibrium will be approximately 1 pp above equilibrium six months later."

    "## 3.4 Parameter Estimation: Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)\n",
    "\n",
    "> **Framework** | **What** | **Why** | **How**\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### 🔷 Framework\n",
    "\n",
    "Given a parametric model with known distributional form, Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) finds the parameter values that make the observed data most probable. Because the OU process has an exact Gaussian transition density, MLE is both feasible and optimal for our setting — it yields consistent, asymptotically efficient estimates with known standard errors.\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### 📌 What\n",
    "\n",
    "For discretely observed data with equal time spacing $\\Delta t = 1/12$ (monthly), the exact transition density gives:\n",
    "\n",
    "$$\n",
    "S_{t_{i+1}}^c \\mid S_{t_i}^c \\sim \\mathcal{N}\\!\\left(\\mu_i^c,\\;\\gamma^{c\\,2}\\right)\n",
    "$$\n",
    "\n",
    "$$\n",
    "\\mu_i^c = \\theta^c + \\left(S_{t_i}^c - \\theta^c\\right)e^{-\\kappa^c \\Delta t}, \\qquad\n",
    "\\gamma^{c\\,2} = \\frac{(\\sigma^c)^2}{2\\kappa^c}\\left(1 - e^{-2\\kappa^c \\Delta t}\\right)\n",
    "$$\n",
    "\n",
    "Given $n$ transitions, the log-likelihood is:\n",
    "\n",
    "$$\n",
    "\\ell\\!\\left(\\kappa^c, \\theta^c, \\sigma^c\\right) = -\\frac{n}{2}\\ln(2\\pi) - \\frac{n}{2}\\ln\\!\\left(\\gamma^{c\\,2}\\right) - \\sum_{i=1}^{n}\\frac{\\left(S_{t_i}^c - \\mu_{i-1}^c\\right)^2}{2\\,\\gamma^{c\\,2}}\n",
    "$$\n",
    "\n",
    "We additionally cross-check via the **AR(1) equivalence**: the OU process observed at discrete intervals is equivalent to:\n",
    "\n",
    "$$\n",
    "S_{t+1}^c = a^c + b^c S_t^c + \\varepsilon_t^c, \\qquad \\varepsilon_t^c \\sim \\mathcal{N}(0, \\gamma^{c\\,2})\n",
    "$$\n",
    "\n",
    "| AR(1) Parameter | OU Equivalent |\n",
    "|:---------------:|:-------------:|\n",
    "| $b^c = e^{-\\kappa^c \\Delta t}$ | $\\kappa^c = -\\frac{\\ln b^c}{\\Delta t}$ |\n",
    "| $a^c = \\theta^c(1 - b^c)$ | $\\theta^c = \\frac{a^c}{1 - b^c}$ |\n",
    "| $\\text{Var}(\\varepsilon^c)$ | $\\sigma^c = \\sqrt{\\frac{2\\kappa^c \\cdot \\text{Var}(\\varepsilon^c)}{1 - (b^c)^2}}$ |\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### ❓ Why\n",
    "\n",
    "- **MLE is optimal for Gaussian models**: it is consistent, asymptotically unbiased, and achieves the Cramér–Rao lower bound. No simulation is required.\n",
    "- **Exact transition density**: unlike approximate methods (e.g., Euler–Maruyama discretization), the Gaussian transition density is exact for the OU process, so our likelihood is free of discretization bias.\n",
    "- **AR(1) cross-check**: simple OLS regression of $S_{t+1}$ on $S_t$ provides an independent validation. Agreement between MLE and AR(1)-derived parameters adds credibility and helps detect numerical optimization failures.\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### ⚙️ How\n",
    "\n",
    "1. Define the negative log-likelihood function `neg_loglik(params, spread, dt)` for each currency.\n",
    "2. Maximize via `scipy.optimize.minimize` using the **L-BFGS-B** method (or Nelder-Mead as fallback), with positivity constraints on $\\kappa$ and $\\sigma$.\n",
    "3. Recover MLE estimates $\\hat{\\kappa}^c$, $\\hat{\\theta}^c$, $\\hat{\\sigma}^c$.\n",
    "4. Compute standard errors from the inverse Hessian (Fisher information matrix) at the optimum. Construct 95% confidence intervals as $\\hat{\\psi} \\pm 1.96 \\cdot \\text{SE}(\\hat{\\psi})$.\n",
    "5. Cross-check via OLS: regress $S_{t+1}$ on $S_t$ using `statsmodels.OLS`; convert AR(1) coefficients $(a, b)$ to OU parameters using the mapping in the table above.\n",
    "6. Flag any discrepancy between MLE and AR(1) estimates exceeding 10% as a diagnostic warning."

    "## 3.5 Crisis Threshold Definition\n",
    "\n",
    "> **Framework** | **What** | **Why** | **How**\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### 🔷 Framework\n",
    "\n",
    "A credit risk index requires a reference point — a threshold above which spread conditions are classified as abnormal or crisis-level. Rather than imposing an arbitrary absolute threshold, we derive thresholds empirically from each currency's own historical distribution, anchoring the definition of \"crisis\" in observed data.\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### 📌 What\n",
    "\n",
    "For each currency, we define the crisis threshold $S_c^c$ as the **95th percentile** of the historical spread distribution:\n",
    "\n",
    "$$\n",
    "S_c^{\\text{USD}} = P_{95}\\!\\left(S_t^{\\text{USD}}\\right), \\qquad S_c^{\\text{KHR}} = P_{95}\\!\\left(S_t^{\\text{KHR}}\\right)\n",
    "$$\n",
    "\n",
    "Sensitivity thresholds at the **90th and 99th percentiles** are also computed for robustness analysis.\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### ❓ Why\n",
    "\n",
    "- **Currency-specific thresholds are essential**: KHR spreads are structurally wider than USD spreads due to the exchange rate risk premium embedded in riel lending. A single absolute threshold applied to both currencies would be economically meaningless — what is \"normal\" for KHR would register as \"crisis\" for USD.\n",
    "- **Empirical calibration**: The 95th percentile anchors the crisis threshold to the actual historical distribution of each currency rather than to an externally imposed absolute level, which would be arbitrary in Cambodia's context.\n",
    "- **Sensitivity analysis** at 90th and 99th percentiles verifies that the CRI's qualitative behavior and the relative risk ranking of USD vs. KHR are robust to threshold choice.\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### ⚙️ How\n",
    "\n",
    "1. For each currency series `spread_usd` and `spread_khr`, compute: `threshold_usd = np.percentile(spread_usd, 95)` and `threshold_khr = np.percentile(spread_khr, 95)`.\n",
    "2. Store the primary (95th) and alternative (90th, 99th) thresholds in a dictionary for downstream use.\n",
    "3. Plot horizontal threshold lines on the spread time series charts to visually confirm that the threshold correctly identifies historically stressed periods."

    "## 3.6 Crisis Probability\n",
    "\n",
    "> **Framework** | **What** | **Why** | **How**\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### 🔷 Framework\n",
    "\n",
    "Rather than a binary signal (\"in crisis\" or \"not in crisis\"), a probabilistic approach provides a continuous, time-varying measure of how close the system is to crisis conditions. The Gaussian structure of the OU model makes this probability analytically computable at every point in time without simulation.\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### 📌 What\n",
    "\n",
    "Using the OU model's Gaussian transition density, we compute the probability that the spread exceeds the crisis threshold at each time $t$:\n",
    "\n",
    "$$\n",
    "P\\!\\left(S_t^c > S_c^c\\right) = 1 - \\Phi\\!\\left(\\frac{S_c^c - m^c(t)}{\\sqrt{v^c(t)}}\\right)\n",
    "$$\n",
    "\n",
    "where:\n",
    "- $\\Phi(\\cdot)$ is the standard normal CDF\n",
    "- $m^c(t)$ is the conditional mean of the spread at time $t$ (from Section 3.3)\n",
    "- $v^c(t)$ is the conditional variance at time $t$ (from Section 3.3)\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### ❓ Why\n",
    "\n",
    "- **Continuous risk signal**: a probability smoothly interpolates between \"low risk\" and \"high risk\", providing more information than a binary crisis flag and making it easier to track the build-up and unwinding of credit stress over time.\n",
    "- **Model-consistency**: the probability is derived directly from the OU model's own distributional assumptions, ensuring it is internally consistent with the parameter estimates.\n",
    "- **No simulation required**: the analytical Gaussian CDF formula eliminates Monte Carlo noise and computational overhead, making the approach fast and reproducible.\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### ⚙️ How\n",
    "\n",
    "1. At each month $t$, compute $m^c(t)$ and $v^c(t)$ using the fitted parameters $(\\hat{\\kappa}^c, \\hat{\\theta}^c, \\hat{\\sigma}^c)$ and the current spread level $S_t^c$ as the starting condition.\n",
    "2. Compute the standardized distance to threshold: `z = (threshold_c - m) / np.sqrt(v)`.\n",
    "3. Compute crisis probability: `prob_crisis = 1 - scipy.stats.norm.cdf(z)`.\n",
    "4. Store the resulting time series `prob_crisis_usd` and `prob_crisis_khr`.\n",
    "5. Verify that crisis probability is high (close to 1) during known stress episodes (e.g., COVID-19 peak) and low (close to 0) during calm periods."

    "## 3.7 Currency-Specific Credit Risk Index (CRI)\n",
    "\n",
    "> **Framework** | **What** | **Why** | **How**\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### 🔷 Framework\n",
    "\n",
    "A composite index that combines multiple risk dimensions captures credit risk more completely than any single measure alone. The CRI is designed to reflect two complementary aspects of risk: the inherent volatility of the spread environment and the proximity of current conditions to historically extreme levels. This dual-component structure is inspired by financial stress indices used in the literature (e.g., the Kansas City Fed's FSI).\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### 📌 What\n",
    "\n",
    "For each currency $c$, the Credit Risk Index is defined as:\n",
    "\n",
    "$$\n",
    "\\text{CRI}^c = \\alpha \\cdot \\hat{\\sigma}^c_{\\text{norm}} + \\beta \\cdot P\\!\\left(S_t^c > S_c^c\\right)\n",
    "$$\n",
    "\n",
    "where:\n",
    "- $\\hat{\\sigma}^c_{\\text{norm}}$ is the OU volatility parameter normalized to $[0,1]$ across currencies\n",
    "- $P(S_t^c > S_c^c)$ is the model-implied crisis probability from Section 3.6\n",
    "- **Baseline weights**: $\\alpha = \\beta = 0.5$ (equal weight to both dimensions)\n",
    "\n",
    "Each component captures a distinct dimension:\n",
    "\n",
    "| Component | Captures | High Value Signals |\n",
    "|-----------|----------|-------------------|\n",
    "| $\\hat{\\sigma}^c_{\\text{norm}}$ | Spread volatility — how erratically spreads move | Unstable risk environment |\n",
    "| $P(S_t^c > S_c^c)$ | Crisis proximity — how close to historically extreme levels | Imminent credit stress |\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### ❓ Why\n",
    "\n",
    "- **Volatility alone is insufficient**: a period of stably high spreads would not trigger high volatility even though it reflects persistent elevated risk. Crisis probability captures this dimension.\n",
    "- **Crisis probability alone is insufficient**: a period of rapidly fluctuating spreads (high volatility) may not yet have breached the threshold. Volatility captures the instability before the threshold is crossed.\n",
    "- **Equal weighting as baseline**: in the absence of a strong prior reason to favor one component, equal weights provide a neutral baseline. Robustness checks ($\\alpha = 0.3, \\beta = 0.7$ and $\\alpha = 0.7, \\beta = 0.3$) verify that findings are not weight-sensitive.\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### ⚙️ How\n",
    "\n",
    "1. Normalize $\\hat{\\sigma}^c$ across the two currencies using min-max normalization: `sigma_norm = (sigma - sigma_min) / (sigma_max - sigma_min)`.\n",
    "2. Compute `CRI_c = 0.5 * sigma_norm_c + 0.5 * prob_crisis_c` for each currency.\n",
    "3. Produce time series plots of `CRI_usd` and `CRI_khr` on the same axis for direct visual comparison.\n",
    "4. Repeat with alternative weights $(0.3, 0.7)$ and $(0.7, 0.3)$ for robustness."

    "## 3.8 System-Wide Composite CRI\n",
    "\n",
    "> **Framework** | **What** | **Why** | **How**\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### 🔷 Framework\n",
    "\n",
    "Policy makers and financial regulators require a single system-level indicator of banking sector credit risk, not two separate currency-specific indicators. Aggregating the currency CRIs into a system-wide index requires a weighting scheme that reflects the economic importance of each currency segment.\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### 📌 What\n",
    "\n",
    "The System CRI is a weighted average of the two currency CRIs:\n",
    "\n",
    "$$\n",
    "\\text{CRI}_{\\text{System}} = w_{\\text{USD}} \\cdot \\text{CRI}^{\\text{USD}} + w_{\\text{KHR}} \\cdot \\text{CRI}^{\\text{KHR}}\n",
    "$$\n",
    "\n",
    "Three weighting schemes are considered:\n",
    "\n",
    "| Weighting Approach | $w_{\\text{USD}}$ | $w_{\\text{KHR}}$ | Rationale |\n",
    "|:------------------:|:----------------:|:----------------:|----------|\n",
    "| **Loan-share weighted** *(primary)* | ~0.80 | ~0.20 | Reflects actual composition of Cambodia's banking system |\n",
    "| Equal weighted | 0.50 | 0.50 | Gives equal attention to both currency segments |\n",
    "| Time-varying | Changes annually | Changes annually | Captures the gradual de-dollarization trend |\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### ❓ Why\n",
    "\n",
    "- **Loan-share weighting is the primary specification** because it reflects economic reality: a shock to USD lending (~80% of the system) has a far larger systemic impact than an equivalent shock to KHR lending (~20%). A system-level index must be proportional to actual exposure.\n",
    "- **Equal weighting** serves as a robustness check and is appropriate from a supervisory standpoint — both currency segments matter regardless of size for financial stability monitoring.\n",
    "- **Time-varying weighting** captures Cambodia's ongoing de-dollarization policy and prevents the index from ignoring the growing KHR share in later years.\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### ⚙️ How\n",
    "\n",
    "1. Obtain annual loan-share weights from NBC statistical bulletins: $w_{\\text{USD},t}$ and $w_{\\text{KHR},t} = 1 - w_{\\text{USD},t}$.\n",

    "\n",
    "> **Framework** | **What** | **Why** | **How**\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### 🔷 Framework\n",
    "\n",
    "Stress testing is a standard tool in prudential risk management (mandated under Basel III/IV and IMF financial sector assessment programs) for evaluating how a risk indicator responds to hypothetical adverse shocks.     "### 📌 What\n",
    "\n",
    "We apply multiplicative stress shocks to the observed spreads:\n",

    "$$\n",
    "\n",
    "Three stress scenarios are applied:\n",
    "\n",
    "| Scenario | Shock ($\\delta$) | Economic Interpretation |\n",
    "|:--------:|:----------------:|------------------------|\n",

    "## 3.13 Regime-Switching Ornstein–Uhlenbeck Model\n",
    "\n",
    "> **Framework** | **What** | **Why** | **How**\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### 🔷 Framework\n",
    "\n",
    "The standard OU model assumes constant parameters throughout the entire sample. In reality, financial systems often alternate between distinct regimes — \"calm\" and \"stress\" — with different dynamics in each. The Markov Regime-Switching model (Hamilton, 1989) extends the OU framework by allowing parameters to shift between latent states, capturing structural breaks without requiring the researcher to prespecify their timing.\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### 📌 What\n",
    "\n",
    "We extend to a **two-state Markov Regime-Switching OU (RS-OU) model**:\n",
    "\n",
    "$$\n",
    "dS_t^c = \\kappa_{s_t}^c\\!\\left(\\theta_{s_t}^c - S_t^c\\right)dt + \\sigma_{s_t}^c\\,dW_t^c, \\qquad s_t \\in \\{1, 2\\}\n",
    "$$\n",
    "\n",
    "where the latent state $s_t$ follows a first-order Markov chain with transition probability matrix:\n",
    "\n",
    "$$\n",
    "\\mathbf{P} = \\begin{pmatrix} p_{11} & 1 - p_{11} \\\\ 1 - p_{22} & p_{22} \\end{pmatrix}\n",
    "$$\n",
    "\n",
    "- **State 1** (\"Calm\"): lower volatility, stable spread dynamics\n",
    "- **State 2** (\"Stress\"): higher volatility, potentially different mean-reversion behavior\n",
    "\n",
    "Model selection between the single-regime OU and the RS-OU is based on the **Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC)**.\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### ❓ Why\n",
    "\n",
    "- **The standard OU model may mask regime heterogeneity**: if calm and stress periods follow different dynamics, pooling them into a single set of parameters produces biased estimates and misleading risk signals.\n",
    "- **Endogenous break detection**: unlike the COVID sub-period analysis (Section 3.10), which fixes regime boundaries exogenously, the RS-OU model lets the data determine when regime switches occur — providing an independent check on the timing of structural breaks.\n",
    "- **Do USD and KHR share the same regime structure?** If the KHR segment switches between calm and stress regimes more frequently, this corroborates the finding of higher KHR instability from the standard OU analysis using a fully different methodology.\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### ⚙️ How\n",
    "\n",
    "1. Define the RS-OU likelihood using the **Hamilton filter** (forward pass): iteratively compute filtered state probabilities $P(s_t = j | S_{1:t})$ using the Gaussian emission densities from the OU model in each state.\n",
    "2. Apply the **Kim smoother** (backward pass) to obtain smoothed probabilities $P(s_t = j | S_{1:T})$ using the full sample.\n",
    "3. Maximize the full log-likelihood over $(\\kappa_1, \\theta_1, \\sigma_1, \\kappa_2, \\theta_2, \\sigma_2, p_{11}, p_{22})$ using `scipy.optimize.minimize`.\n",
    "4. Compare BIC of the RS-OU against the single-regime OU: if $\\text{BIC}_{\\text{RS-OU}} < \\text{BIC}_{\\text{OU}}$, the regime-switching extension is supported by the data.\n",
    "5. Plot smoothed state probabilities alongside the spread series to identify when each currency operated in the stress regime."

    "## 3.15 Implied Default Probability\n",
    "\n",
    "> **Framework** | **What** | **Why** | **How**\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### 🔷 Framework\n",
    "\n",
    "Reduced-form credit risk models (Duffie & Singleton, 1999; Jarrow & Turnbull, 1995) link observed credit spreads to implied default intensities via a no-arbitrage pricing identity: the spread compensates lenders for expected losses (default probability × loss given default). This identity allows us to extract market-implied default probabilities from the observed spreads, translating an abstract index into a concrete, economically interpretable quantity.\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### 📌 What\n",
    "\n",
    "Under the assumption that the spread compensates for expected losses:\n",
    "\n",
    "$$\n",
    "S_t^c \\approx h_t^c \\cdot (1 - R^c)\n",
    "$$\n",
    "\n",
    "where $h_t^c$ is the instantaneous hazard rate (default intensity) and $R^c$ is the recovery rate (baseline assumption: $R = 0.40$, the standard Basel assumption).\n",
    "\n",
    "The extracted hazard rate and 1-year cumulative default probability are:\n",
    "\n",
    "$$\n",
    "h_t^c = \\frac{S_t^c}{1 - R^c}, \\qquad\n",
    "\\text{PD}_{1\\text{yr}}^c(t) = 1 - \\exp\\!\\left(-h_t^c\\right)\n",
    "$$\n",
    "\n",
    "Recovery rate sensitivity is assessed across $R \\in \\{0.20, 0.30, 0.40, 0.50, 0.60\\}$.\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### ❓ Why\n",
    "\n",
    "- **Concrete interpretation**: while the CRI is a dimensionless index, the implied PD is expressed in probability units — directly comparable to non-performing loan ratios, regulatory risk weights, and external credit rating benchmarks.\n",
    "- **Bridging CRI to existing risk frameworks**: policymakers familiar with Basel-style default probability metrics can immediately relate the CRI signal to standard credit risk terminology.\n",

    "\n",
    "> **Framework** | **What** | **Why** | **How**\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### 🔷 Framework\n",
    "\n",
    "Vector Autoregression (VAR) models the joint dynamics of multiple time series without imposing a priori restrictions on which variables cause which. Developed by Sims (1980) as an atheoretical alternative to structural macroeconomic models, VAR is the standard tool for examining transmission channels and dynamic interdependencies between macroeconomic and financial variables. In this context, it allows us to test empirically whether Cambodia's dollarization effectively transmits U.S. monetary policy into domestic credit risk conditions.\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### 📌 What\n",
    "\n",
    "We employ a VAR model with the following variable vector:\n",
    "\n",
    "$$\n",
    "\\mathbf{Y}_t = \\mathbf{c} + \\sum_{j=1}^{p} \\mathbf{A}_j \\mathbf{Y}_{t-j} + \\mathbf{u}_t, \\qquad\n",
    "\\mathbf{Y}_t = \\begin{pmatrix} S_t^{\\text{USD}} \\\\ S_t^{\\text{KHR}} \\\\ \\text{FedFunds}_t \\end{pmatrix}\n",
    "$$\n",
    "\n",
    "where $p$ is the optimal lag order selected by the BIC. Three analytical tools are applied:\n",
    "\n",
    "| Tool | Question Answered |\n",
    "|------|------------------|\n",
    "| **Granger Causality Tests** | Does the Fed Funds Rate Granger-cause USD or KHR spreads? Does one currency's spread predict the other's? |\n",
    "| **Impulse Response Functions (IRFs)** | How do USD and KHR spreads respond dynamically to a one-SD shock in the Fed Funds Rate? How long does the effect persist? |\n",
    "| **Variance Decomposition** | What share of forecast error variance in each spread is attributable to Fed policy shocks vs. innovations in the other currency's spread? |\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### ❓ Why\n",
    "\n",
    "- **Testing the \"monetary policy outsourcing\" hypothesis**: Cambodia's lack of an independent monetary policy instrument (due to high dollarization) is a central policy concern.     "- **Granger causality reveals information flow**: knowing whether Fed policy leads or lags domestic credit spreads helps NBC anticipate when to deploy macroprudential tools.\n",
    "- **IRFs quantify the duration and magnitude** of external shocks on domestic credit conditions — directly relevant for stress scenario design and macroprudential policy timing.\n",
    "- **VAR is appropriate for non-structural policy analysis**: without imposing a specific structural model, VAR allows the data to reveal the empirical transmission dynamics.\n",
    "\n",
    "---\n",
    "\n",
    "### ⚙️ How\n",
    "\n",
    "1. **Pre-estimation tests**: apply ADF unit root tests to each variable; difference as needed to achieve stationarity. Test for cointegration if variables are integrated.\n",
